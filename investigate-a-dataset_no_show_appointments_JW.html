1. Introduction¶

If a person makes a doctor's appointment, receives instructions and does not turn up, who is at fault? A no-show appointment leads to missed opportunities for other patients and an inefficient use of doctors and nurses.

The purpose of this task is to determine whether we can predict when a no-show appointment will occur.

The data set contains information on 110,527 medical appointments from different parts of Brazil and focuses on the question of whether or not patients show up for their appointments and how the attributes that are about to be presented influence this probability.

The dataset contains 14 attributes:

  • PatientID - Identification of the patient
  • AppointmentID - Identification of each appointment
  • Gender - Male or Female (M/F)
  • ScheduledDay - The day of the appointment
  • AppointmentDay - The day the appointment was made
  • Age - The age of the patient
  • Neighbourhood - Where the appointment takes place
  • Scholarship - True or False
  • Hipertension - True or False
  • Diabetes - True or False
  • Alcoholism - True or False
  • Handicap - There are 5 different levels from 0 - 4
  • SMS_received - 1 or more messages sent to the patient
  • No-show - Yes or No. Yes indicates that the patient did not turn up for the appointment.

Potential questions:

  • What gender (M/F) is most likely to not attend an appointment?
  • How does age affect whether a patient will turn up to an appointment?
  • What impact does the location of the hospital have on appointment attendance?
  • How does the number of days between making an appointment to the scheduled date, affect attendance?
  • How effective are SMS reminders?
  • How does day of the week affect attendance?

Key Findings and Observations from the Data Wrangling process:¶

  • The no show appointments dataset contains 110,527 records with 14 columns.
  • Two column headers contain spelling mistakes - these are 'Hipertension' and 'Handcap'.
  • ScheduledDay and AppointmentDay columns are of a string datatype, however datetime format would be more appropriate.
  • There are no rows with duplicate data.
  • There are no rows will null values.
  • There are 48,228 duplicated Patient Id's. This suggests that patients can make more than 1 appointment.
  • Of the 104 unique age values, there is an age of -1 which does not appear to be correct.
  • As there is such a wide range of ages, we will create bin edges that will be used to "cut" the data into groups.
  • Appointment ID and Patient ID columns do not appear to add much value, these can be removed.
  • The focus of this analysis will be on all other remaining columns.

5. Conclusions¶

From the dataset available and analysis performed, the following conclusions can be made:

  • Gender does not appear to affect whether a patient will turn up to an appointment. While the dataset was approximately 65% female, the same proportion of males and females are likely to miss an appointment (approximately 20%).
  • The proportion of missed appointments is highest amongst the 18-35 year old age group at 24%. This could be perhaps they are busy at school, University, work or taking care of their family.
  • The location of the hospital/medical centre does not appear to significantly affect attendance.
  • The number of days between when the appointment is made and the scheduled date appears to be the most indicative. For appointments made on the same day, it is highly likely the patient will turn up. Most likely because it may be an urgent appointment. Appointments made more than 7 days in advance have a high proportion of no-shows, either the patient forgot, or no longer needs to see a doctor and forgot to cancel the appointment.
  • Of the SMS reminders sent out to patients, approximately 27% of patients still missed their appointment.
  • The day of the week does not appear to affect attendance.

Limitations:

  • The dataset is only limited to a portion of the year. To gain further insights, it would be better to have a dataset over a year to see if there are any seasonal trends and to provide a larger dataset.
  • The dataset could include further information on patients (eg: highest education, annual household income)
  • The dataset could include further information on the hospital/medical centre (eg: price, region, social-economic factors).
